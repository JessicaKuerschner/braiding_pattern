Between 1811 and 1812, these workers rose up — with an intensity far beyond that of the Robin Hood legends. Their attacks were coordinated, disciplined, and effective. Entire networks of support shielded them for months, despite police spies, informants, and military repression. The government deployed more soldiers to suppress them than were fighting Napoleon at the time. The Luddites were not only seen as a threat to public order — they were perceived as a threat to industrial progress itself. Their struggle questioned the moral legitimacy of a new economic order in which capital determined all, and where ancient communal rights could simply be erased. Industrialism, he argues, is inseparable from the extraction of resources, the dispossession of common lands, and the loss of autonomy among agricultural and craft communities.

For a long time, machine-breaking was dismissed as irrational, backward, or anti-progress. Marx himself viewed the Luddites as politically naive, unable to distinguish between the machine as an object and the capitalist system that controlled it. But this view misses something essential. The Luddites did not reject technology. They rejected a technology that destroys skill, autonomy, and meaning in work. The shift from handcraft to machinery was not simply a matter of efficiency — it was a cultural and political transformation. The machine did not support the worker; the worker now served the machine.

This is what we call technology today: technique detached from tradition, community, and human scale, technique that dictates social life rather than serving it. With industrialization, the political realm shrank. Decisions about how we live were replaced by the supposed inevitability of technological progress. The future became something imposed, not chosen.

The Luddites resisted this, and paid for it dearly. Yet their struggle still matters. The refusal to let machines determine the shape of life reappears today in movements resisting digital surveillance, AI automation, 5G infrastructure, ecological destruction, and the expansion of data and extraction networks. Machine sabotage has again become a political act, a way to interrupt a future no one asked for.

We started this project by willing to have a technocritical position towards technology. A fertile technology is a technology who set free. A convivial tool is a tool which shouldn't remove your capacity of doing, creates neither slaves or master, should enlarge your scope of individual action. Machines are itself interlocutors and we should learn how to speak with them. If they simule dialog, we should simulate respect and ethics, and treat her the way we would like her to treat her. If we happen to live now with technology, then we need to learn how to treat it like a "convive", etymologically, the one that is living with us. Escaping form inter-human weaved connection and rather going towards with braided connections between humans and machines?

Why Braiding

Braiding is polygenetic, meaning it can't be traced back to a single origin culture. Traditionally, it's a social art: braiding takes time, and that time becomes a space for connection. Elders braid while teaching, children observe and practice on one another, and in this way, designs, and relationships, are passed down through generations.

Our machine draws inspiration from the maypole braiding machine, itself rooted in an old tradition: people dancing around a pole in spring, weaving patterns as a collective act of joy, fertility, and shared rhythm. What stands out is the shared responsibility, every participant is equally involved in creating the braid.

Historically, braiding materials were always tied to place, drawn from the plants and animals native to the land.

At Corbeo, a rope factory in Belgium, the production floor is operated by few human hands only. Most steps are automated. Yet, in the rope braiding section, the human hand is still a necessity. One worker operates several machines - winding, unwinding, braiding. Fine motor skills are required in several steps. Tying old yarns to a new one, cutting yarn from the spool, integrating the new yarn into the rope…

An engineer also explained to us how the mechanism works and how the rope making changed over the course of three generations.

In Eindhoven, at Café Wilhelmina, we observed the system of traditional dance, with its people, patterns and rhythms. The Balfolk community comes together through a shared etiquette, a code of conduct and through each step. Some dances are more intense and energetic than others, which are slower paced. With each dance a stronger connection is made.

MACHINE Mechanism

The Website

On our website, we present the development process openly, following an open-source logic. Our process, how to build the machine and the machine's interactive features are shared, allowing others to learn, replicate, or modify them as well as interact with each other.

How to interact with the machine

The machine is designed for hybrid interaction. Physical and digital inputs work together: one supports and responds to the other. A code of conduct guides how people in the physical space and users online engage with the machine. Both sides influence the final result, creating a shared experience across spaces.

Function Physical Interaction Digital Interaction Collaboration Effect
Speed & Rhythm Physical participants move, step, tap in time using foot pedals, sensors, or motion tracking Online participants send rhythmic signals that merge with physical rhythm Creating a common tempo: physical and digital rhythms merge into shared timing
Color Application Press color pads, apply pigment as threads move Select color palettes through digital interface Watch, wait, respond—coordination between physical and digital color choices
Material Choice Change modular components (rings, spools, guides)—like changing dance partners Digital interface suggests new material combinations Digital input guides physical selection, creating dialogue
Structure & Pattern Alter thread paths by repositioning components Propose pattern variations digitally Combined influence creates hybrid patterns—neither purely physical nor purely digital
Feedback & Response See, touch, hear the MACHINE—direct sensory experience View live visual data (speed, tension, sound frequency) Digital users interpret and respond to sensory feedback, creating continuous dialogue

What is the outcome

There is no fixed outcome. The aim is to create a system with enough flexibility to allow for experimentation. The structure invites users to build, test, and adapt within context. What emerges depends on the tools, people, and conditions involved—leaving space for new possibilities to be discovered during use.
