Our Process

Workshop: We found a link between maypole dancing and rope making. Because essentially you are making a rope (on top of a tree stem) through the dance. We tried out different forms: the tent maypole, single plait maypole, and full pleat maypole. We also practiced the Branle de l'Official, a folk dance.

Visit Factory: We explored the relationship between machine vs. worker, examining questions of agency. We observed the process from thread to rope. We saw how workers interact with machines—compared to other machines, workers are definitely needed for some tasks because they're so intricate.

Folk Dance: There are

  • First dance: very little body contact (just holding on one finger), dance in row so you can see everyone once
  • Second dance: choose randomly one partner that's next to you, but not too close body contact
  • Third dance: ask some partner to dance with you

Research Question

How can braiding be reimagined as both a technological and social system, where new tools and community activation make production more accessible, creative, poetic and collaborative?

Machine Mechanism

Element Braiding Machine Social Dance
Interdependence Each bobbin's movement depends on others Each person relies on the group
Rhythm & Timing Mechanism needs precise, rhythmic motion Music and rhythm guide synchronized gestures
Pattern Formation Crossing threads creates structured patterns How people cross paths forms spatial and temporal patterns
Tension Balanced tension between threads Spatial distance and connection between dancers
Continuity Can operate continuously; starts/ends when worker chooses Can continue indefinitely; begins/ends when community decides
Rules & Codes Mechanical constraints and physical laws Social codes of conduct and consent
Spatial Organization Bobbins follow predetermined paths Dancers move in geometric patterns, choreographed yet improvised
Embodied Knowledge Traditional craft techniques passed down Traditional steps, collective memory—a living archive
Sound Mechanical noise, rhythmic patterns Music, footsteps, breath
Interaction Worker responds to machine errors—relationship is responsive, not hierarchical People respond to each other—relationship is responsive, not hierarchical

Function: Physical Interaction, Digital Interaction, Collaboration Effect

Function Physical Interaction Digital Interaction Collaboration Effect
Speed & Rhythm Physical participants move, step, tap in time using foot pedals, sensors, or motion tracking Online participants send rhythmic signals that merge with physical rhythm Creating a common tempo: physical and digital rhythms merge into shared timing
Color Application Press color pads, apply pigment as threads move Select color palettes through digital interface Watch, wait, respond—coordination between physical and digital color choices
Material Choice Change modular components (rings, spools, guides)—like changing dance partners Digital interface suggests new material combinations Digital input guides physical selection, creating dialogue
Structure & Pattern Alter thread paths by repositioning components Propose pattern variations digitally Combined influence creates hybrid patterns—neither purely physical nor purely digital
Feedback & Response See, touch, hear the machine—direct sensory experience View live visual data (speed, tension, sound frequency) Digital users interpret and respond to sensory feedback, creating continuous dialogue
